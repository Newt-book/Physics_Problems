# Velocity Transformation

## Given

Rocket speed relative to Earth:

\[
v = 0.8c
\]

Probe speed relative to the rocket:

\[
u = 0.5c
\]

We want to determine the speed of the probe as measured by an observer on Earth using:

1. Classical Mechanics
2. Special Relativity

---

# Part A: Classical Mechanics

According to classical mechanics, velocities simply add together.

The velocity addition formula is:

\[
u' = u + v
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
u' = 0.5c + 0.8c
\]

\[
u' = 1.3c
\]

### Classical Answer

\[
\boxed{u' = 1.3c}
\]

---

# Limitation of Classical Mechanics

This result predicts that the probe travels faster than the speed of light.

However, Einstein's theory of Special Relativity states that no object can travel faster than:

\[
c
\]

Therefore, the classical result is physically impossible.

This shows that classical mechanics is not valid at speeds close to the speed of light.

---

# Part B: Special Relativity

Einstein's velocity transformation formula is:

\[
u' = \frac{u+v}{1+\frac{uv}{c^2}}
\]

Substituting:

\[
u = 0.5c
\]

\[
v = 0.8c
\]

---

## Step 1: Calculate the Numerator

\[
u+v
=
0.5c+0.8c
\]

\[
=1.3c
\]

---

## Step 2: Calculate the Denominator

\[
1+\frac{uv}{c^2}
\]

\[
1+\frac{(0.5c)(0.8c)}{c^2}
\]

\[
1+0.4
\]

\[
1.4
\]

---

## Step 3: Calculate the Final Velocity

\[
u'
=
\frac{1.3c}{1.4}
\]

\[
u'
=
0.9286c
\]

\[
u'
\approx0.929c
\]

### Relativistic Answer

\[
\boxed{u' \approx 0.929c}
\]

---

# Comparison of Results

| Method | Speed of Probe |
|----------|----------|
| Classical Mechanics | \(1.3c\) |
| Special Relativity | \(0.929c\) |

---

# Discussion

The classical result predicts a speed greater than the speed of light, which is impossible according to modern physics.

Special Relativity provides a corrected velocity transformation formula that ensures no object can exceed the speed of light.

At everyday speeds, the difference between classical and relativistic calculations is extremely small. However, at speeds close to the speed of light, relativistic effects become significant and must be considered.
